## How to Identify the Essay Type

| Question phrasing clue | Essay type |
|------------------------|-----------|
| "Do you agree or disagree?" | Opinion |
| "To what extent do you agree or disagree?" | Opinion |
| "What is your opinion?" | Opinion |
| "Discuss both views and give your opinion." | Discussion |
| "Some people think X, others think Y. Discuss." | Discussion |
| "What are the causes/reasons? What solutions can be suggested?" | Problem & Solution |
| "What are the problems and solutions?" | Problem & Solution |
| "What are the advantages and disadvantages?" | Advantages & Disadvantages |
| "Do the advantages outweigh the disadvantages?" | Advantages & Disadvantages (with opinion) |
| "Is this a positive or negative development?" | Advantages & Disadvantages (with opinion) |
| Two separate questions in one prompt | Two-Part Question |

## Type 1: Opinion (Agree/Disagree)

**Prompt pattern**: "To what extent do you agree or disagree with this statement?"

### Recommended 4-Paragraph Structure

**Introduction**
- Paraphrase the question (do not copy)
- State your position clearly with a degree word (*strongly, totally, fully, firmly*)
- Templates:
  - *It is often argued that [X]. From my perspective, I fully agree with this view for two main reasons.*
  - *Some people believe that [X]. However, I strongly disagree with this statement because [brief reason].*
  - *The claim that [X] is one that I largely agree with, and this essay will explain why.*

**Body Paragraph 1 — First Reason (TEE method)**
- Topic sentence stating your first reason
- Explanation/elaboration
- Concrete example
- Templates:
  - *First and foremost, [reason]. It is undeniable that [elaboration]. For example, [example].*
  - *One key reason is that [reason]. To be more specific, [elaboration]. This is evident in [example].*

**Body Paragraph 2 — Second Reason OR Concession**

*Option A: Second supporting reason*
- *Secondly/Moreover, [reason]. [Elaboration]. For instance, [example].*

*Option B: Concession + refutation (more sophisticated)*
- *Admittedly, some argue that [opposing view]. To some extent, this argument seems reasonable because [example]. However, [refutation]. Therefore, this does not undermine my overall position.*

**Conclusion**
- No new information
- Rephrase and summarise your position
- Templates:
  - *In conclusion, I firmly believe that [restate position] because [brief summary of reasons].*
  - *In a nutshell, while [concession], I strongly agree that [position] due to [summary].*

## Type 2: Discussion (Discuss Both Views)

**Prompt pattern**: "Discuss both views and give your own opinion."

### Recommended 4-Paragraph Structure

**Introduction**
- Paraphrase the question
- Acknowledge both views exist
- State your own opinion
- Templates:
  - *Some people hold the view that [A], while others argue that [B]. From my perspective, I believe [your opinion].*
  - *There is ongoing debate about whether [A] or [B]. This essay will discuss both perspectives before reaching a conclusion.*

**Body Paragraph 1 — View A (One side)**
- Topic: *On one hand, …*
- Reasons and elaboration
- Example: *at least 1*
- Templates:
  - *On one hand, proponents of [A] argue that [reason]. This is because [elaboration]. For example, [example].*

**Body Paragraph 2 — View B (Other side) + Your Opinion**
- Topic: *On the other hand, …*
- Reasons and elaboration
- Example
- Include your opinion here: *Personally, I find this view more convincing because …*
- Templates:
  - *On the other hand, [B] supporters contend that [reason]. [Elaboration]. For instance, [example]. I find this perspective more persuasive because [your reason].*

**Conclusion**
- Summarise both views
- Restate your opinion
- Templates:
  - *In conclusion, although [View A has merit], I believe that [View B/your opinion] for the reasons outlined above.*
  - *In a nutshell, both perspectives have their merits; however, I am more persuaded by [your view] because [brief reason].*

## Type 3: Problem & Solution

**Prompt patterns**:
- "What are the main causes of this? What solutions can be suggested?"
- "What are the problems caused by X? What measures can be taken?"

### Recommended 4-Paragraph Structure

**Introduction**
- Paraphrase the question
- Outline what the essay will cover (reasons/solutions)
- Templates:
  - *It is true that [X is a problem]. From my perspective, there are various factors contributing to this issue, and there are also effective solutions that can be implemented.*
  - *This essay will delve into the main reasons behind [problem] and propose potential solutions.*

**Body Paragraph 1 — Causes/Reasons**
- Topic: *One main reason/cause is that …*
- Elaboration + result
- Templates:
  - *One primary cause of [problem] is [reason]. As a result, [consequence].*
  - *First of all, [reason]. This leads to [consequence], particularly in [context].*

**Body Paragraph 2 — Solutions**
- Topic: *One primary solution is …*
- Elaboration + expected outcome
- Templates:
  - *One effective solution would be to [measure]. This would [expected outcome].*
  - *Another way to address this issue is to [measure]. By doing so, [positive result].*
  - *Secondly, [second solution]. Because [elaboration], this could [outcome].*

**Conclusion**
- Summarise causes and solutions
- Templates:
  - *In conclusion, [problem] stems primarily from [cause summary]. However, [solution summary] can effectively address these challenges.*
  - *In summary, while [causes], [solutions] offer viable ways to mitigate the issue.*

## Type 4: Advantages & Disadvantages

**Prompt patterns**:
- "What are the advantages and disadvantages of X?"
- "Do the advantages of X outweigh the disadvantages?"
- "Is this a positive or negative development?"

### Recommended 4-Paragraph Structure

**Introduction**
- Paraphrase the question
- For "outweigh" / "positive or negative" type: state your position
- Templates:
  - *There is no doubt that [X has both benefits and drawbacks]. This essay will examine both sides.*
  - *Having said that, I believe the [positive effects/benefits] of [X] outweigh the [negative effects/drawbacks].*
  - *In my view, despite the fact that [disadvantage], I believe the overall impact of [X] is largely positive.*

**Body Paragraph 1 — Advantages**
- Topic: *On one hand, …*
- Reasons: *It is beneficial for … / This is advantageous because …*
- Example
- Templates:
  - *On one hand, [X] offers significant benefits. It is particularly beneficial for [group] because [reason]. For example, [example]. Thus, [conclusion].*

**Body Paragraph 2 — Disadvantages**
- Topic: *However, / On the other hand, …*
- Reasons: *It is detrimental for … / This is problematic because …*
- Example
- For "outweigh" essays: rebut the disadvantage at the end
- Templates:
  - *On the other hand, [X] also brings certain drawbacks. Specifically, [disadvantage]. For instance, [example]. This leads to [negative consequence].*
  - (For outweigh): *However, I would argue that these drawbacks are exceeded by the benefits outlined above, as [brief rebuttal].*

**Conclusion**
- Summarise both sides
- Restate your position (if required)
- Templates:
  - *In conclusion, while [X] presents some [disadvantages], its [advantages] are more significant. I believe the positive effects ultimately outweigh the negative ones.*
  - *In a nutshell, [X] has both merits and limitations; however, I maintain that its [advantages/disadvantages] are more impactful overall.*

## Type 5: Two-Part Question

**Prompt pattern**: Two separate, distinct questions in one prompt.
*Example: "Why has this happened? What can be done about it?"*

### Recommended 4-Paragraph Structure

**Introduction**
- Paraphrase the topic
- Indicate the essay will address both questions
- Template:
  - *There are [several/various] reasons why [topic]. This essay will examine the key factors and also propose [solutions/what can be done].*

**Body Paragraph 1 — Answer Question 1**
- Topic: *Firstly, …*
- Reasons/explanations
- Example
- Templates:
  - *Firstly, [reason A]. This occurs because [elaboration]. For example, [example].*

**Body Paragraph 2 — Answer Question 2**
- Topic: *When it comes to [Question 2], …*
- Reasons/explanations
- Example
- Templates:
  - *When it comes to [Question 2], one important [measure/reason/effect] is [answer]. [Elaboration]. For instance, [example].*

**Conclusion**
- Briefly summarise answers to both questions
- Template:
  - *In conclusion, [summary of Q1 answer], and [summary of Q2 answer]. [Brief final thought].*

## TEE Method

The TEE (Topic–Explain–Example) method is the core structure for every body paragraph. Use it to teach students why paragraphs feel underdeveloped.

```
T — Topic sentence      State the main point of the paragraph
E — Explanation         Elaborate on WHY or HOW — don't just assert
E — Example             Give a concrete, specific example to illustrate
```

**Extended version (TEEC)**:
```
T — Topic
E — Explanation
E — Example
C — Conclusion/Link    Connect back to the main argument or transition forward
```

**Example — Band 7 body paragraph using TEE**:
> *Firstly, social media has significantly undermined face-to-face communication. [T] When people spend hours scrolling through their phones, they become less practised at holding real conversations and may find it difficult to read social cues. [E] For example, studies have found that heavy social media users score lower on empathy tests compared to those who engage in more direct social interaction. [E]*

## Key Discourse Markers by Function

### Adding information
*Furthermore · Moreover · In addition · Additionally · Not only … but also*

### Contrasting
*However · On the other hand · Nevertheless · Despite this · Although · Even though · While*

### Cause & Effect
*As a result · Therefore · Consequently · This leads to · Due to · Because of · This is why*

### Giving examples
*For example · For instance · Such as · A case in point is · To illustrate*

### Conceding a point
*Admittedly · It is true that · While it may be argued that · Although one could argue that … · To some extent*

### Concluding
*In conclusion · In summary · To conclude · Overall · In a nutshell · To sum up*

### Ordering
*First(ly) · Second(ly) · Third(ly) · To begin with · Next · Then · Finally · Last but not least*

### Emphasising
*Particularly · Especially · Above all · Indeed · In fact · Notably*
