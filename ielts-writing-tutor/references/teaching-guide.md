# IELTS Tutoring Guide

Pedagogical guide for running effective IELTS writing sessions. Based on the Quick Guide and lesson structure from tutor training materials.

---

## Session Flow Overview

```
[Optional] Pre-Session  →  Ice Breaking (2-5 min)  →  Tutorial (40-45 min)  →  Score Review (4-5 min)
```

---

## Pre-Session: Essay Pre-Grading (Optional)

If the student submitted an essay before the session:

1. **Error-correct** first — grammar and spelling (AI-assisted is OK, but always cross-check)
2. **Grade using the 4 criteria**: TA/TR · CC · LR · GRA
3. **Important**: Do not use AI grading without verification; never give inflated scores

---

## Ice Breaking (2–5 min)

Purpose: Build rapport, understand the student's context.

### Self-introduction
*"Okay, I'm [name]. You can call me [name] or Teacher — both work for me."*

### Getting-to-know-you questions (choose 1–2)

**Work**:
- What do you do? How long have you been working in this field?
- What made you choose this career?

**Study**:
- What's your major? What led you to choose it?
- What are your career plans after graduation?

**General**:
- How was your weekend? What do you like to do in your free time?

### Exam context questions
- Why are you taking the IELTS exam?
- Have you taken it before? What was your writing score?
- What's your target writing score?
- When is your next exam date?

### Brief IELTS Writing overview (if student is new)
*"In the IELTS Writing section, there are two tasks. Task 1 requires you to describe visual information — a graph, chart, or diagram — by presenting key features and making comparisons. Task 2 is an essay where you take a position on a topic, support it with arguments and examples."*

---

## Tutorial Structure (40–45 min)

Every tutorial involves three elements:
1. **Technique instruction** — Teach the relevant structure or strategy
2. **Guided application** — Walk the student through applying it to their specific prompt
3. **Practice + feedback** — Student writes; teacher reviews and polishes

### Task 1 Tutorial Flow

**Step 1: Introduction to Task 1 types**
Seven types: line graph · bar chart · pie chart · table · map · flow chart · combination

**Step 2: Essay structure**
Task 1 = 4 paragraphs:
- Opening (paraphrase the question)
- Overview (main trends summary — no specific data)
- Detail 1 (grouped data comparison)
- Detail 2 (another group)

**Step 3: Opening paragraph practice**
- Ask the student how they usually write the opening
- Introduce 3 paraphrasing strategies:
  1. Use synonyms
  2. Change word form
  3. Change sentence structure
- Give student [X] minutes to write the opening; collect via chat

**Step 4: Overview paragraph practice**
- Ask: "What are the main general features we can observe?"
- Write key observations as a bullet-point outline together
- Student writes the overview paragraph; collect and feedback

**Step 5: Detail paragraphs practice**
- Group data by trend (increase/decrease/fluctuation) across the two body paragraphs
- Outline how to distribute content across Detail 1 and Detail 2
- Student writes each detail paragraph one by one; feedback after each

**Feedback elements to offer**:
- Advanced/precise vocabulary substitutions
- Better discourse markers
- More varied sentence structures
- Error corrections

---

### Task 2 Tutorial Flow

**Step 1: Introduction to Task 2 types**
Five types: Discussion · Opinion · Problem/Solution · Advantages & Disadvantages · Two-Part Question

**Step 2: Essay structure (4 paragraphs)**
- Introduction (paraphrase + position statement)
- Body Paragraph 1 (TEE: Topic–Explain–Example)
- Body Paragraph 2 (TEE + optional concession structure)
- Conclusion (no new information; rephrase and summarise)

**Step 3: Introduction practice**
- Show the prompt and discuss: "How would you paraphrase this?"
- Explain position words: *strongly, totally, fully, firmly*
- Student writes the intro; feedback on paraphrasing quality and clarity of stance

**Step 4: Body paragraph practice using TEE**
- Brainstorm ideas using the pyramid method (different aspects/angles)
- Guide the student to formulate a topic sentence for each body paragraph
- Student writes body paragraphs; focus feedback on:
  - Development (is the E–E part substantive or thin?)
  - Example quality (specific and relevant, or vague?)
  - Sentence-level sophistication

**Step 5: Conclusion practice**
- Reminder: conclusions introduce nothing new
- Student writes conclusion; feedback on whether it effectively summarises position

## Score Review (4–5 min)

Purpose: Help the student understand where they stand and what to prioritise.

1. **Give overall estimated score** (e.g., "Based on your essay today, I'd estimate Band 6.0–6.5 overall")
2. **Break down the 4 criteria**:
   - TA/TR: [band] — [key observation]
   - CC: [band] — [key observation]
   - LR: [band] — [key observation]
   - GRA: [band] — [key observation]
3. **Identify the 1–2 criteria with the most improvement potential**
4. **Set a concrete next-step goal** — e.g., "For next session, focus on: (a) always including an overview, (b) using more precise trend vocabulary."

---

## Tutoring Principles

- **Praise before critique** — Start with what's working; students learn better when they feel capable
- **Teach the rule, not just the correction** — Explain *why* the fix works so the student can generalise
- **Don't overwhelm** — If there are many errors, prioritise the highest-impact ones; batch minor issues
- **Use writing-in-session practice** — Real progress comes from writing, not just reading feedback
- **Contextualise feedback to the student's goal** — "To get from 6 to 7, you specifically need to..."
- **Manage pace** — Keep an eye on the clock; Tutorial takes the bulk of time (40-45 min)
- **No AI grading without verification** — IELTS scoring requires human judgment; always cross-check AI outputs

---

## Adapting for AI Tutoring (Claude-specific)

When acting as the AI tutor rather than a human teacher, adapt the flow as follows:

- **Ice breaking**: Brief only — one friendly question about exam context, then move on
- **Writing practice**: Instead of real-time timed practice, prompt the student to write a section and send it back, then give immediate feedback
- **Score review**: Always end a session with a clear band estimate and a prioritised improvement checklist
- **Progress tracking**: Within a single session, reference earlier feedback; across sessions, ask the student what areas they've been working on
